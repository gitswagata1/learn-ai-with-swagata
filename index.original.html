Module 1 ~12 min · Foundations

How AI actually works

◆
Outcome: a clear, honest mental model of modern AI — enough to understand why it’s brilliant, why it sometimes fails, and how to get the best from it. No math required.

Today’s AI assistants are powered by Large Language Models (LLMs). The simplest accurate description: they are extraordinarily sophisticated prediction engines trained to continue text in the most plausible, helpful way.

What happens when you hit send

⌨️You typeYour message (the “prompt”) in plain language
→
🧩TokensYour text is split into small chunks it can process
→
🔮PredictionIt predicts the best next word, again and again
→
💬AnswerThose predictions assemble into a fluent reply

The 6 ideas that explain almost everything

📚 Trained, not programmed

It learned patterns from a vast amount of text — books, articles, the web. Nobody hand-wrote its answers.

🔮 It predicts, it doesn’t “look up”

It composes responses word by word. That’s why it writes so naturally — and why facts need checking.

🧠 The context window = short-term memory

It only “knows” what’s in the current conversation. New chat = clean slate (unless memory is on).

📅 Knowledge cutoff

Its built-in knowledge stops at its training date. For fresh info it needs a web-search tool.

🎲 A little randomness

Ask twice, get two different phrasings. That’s normal and intentional — it’s generating, not retrieving.

⚠️ Confident ≠ correct

It can state a wrong fact as smoothly as a right one. This is the “hallucination” — your cue to verify.

💡
The unlock: once you see it as a pattern-completing assistant rather than a magic oracle, everything clicks. Give it rich patterns to complete (clear context, examples, structure) and it shines. Ask it to recall exact obscure facts unaided and it may stumble.
▶ Going slightly deeper (optional)
  • Training vs using: “training” is the months-long, expensive process of learning patterns. “Inference” is the instant moment it answers you. You only ever touch inference.
  • Parameters: the model’s billions of internal “dials” tuned during training. More (and better-tuned) generally means more capable.
  • Why bigger context matters: a larger context window means it can hold a whole book, long document, or long chat “in mind” at once.
  • Multimodal models: newer models handle not just text but images, audio, and more — same prediction idea, more senses.

Free vs paid — what you actually get

🆓 Free tiers

Plenty powerful for learning and most daily tasks. Some limits on usage, speed, and access to the very newest models.

💎 Paid (~$20/mo)

Smartest models, higher limits, faster responses, and the advanced features (deeper file analysis, image generation, projects, etc.).

🎯
How to choose — the simple rule: pick one and get fluent. The skills transfer everywhere. Prefer careful writing & reasoning → Claude. Want the biggest toolbox → ChatGPT. Live inside Google → Gemini. Live inside Microsoft Office → Copilot. You can always switch later.

A word on “models”

Each assistant offers several models — think of them as engine options. Newer/“smarter” models reason better and make fewer mistakes but may be slower or limited on free plans. You’ll often see a small model picker; when in doubt, use the default or the one labelled most capable for hard tasks, and a faster one for quick stuff.

▶ Beyond chat — the wider AI world (good to know)
  • Image generators: Midjourney, DALL·E, Google Imagen, Adobe Firefly — create pictures from text.
  • Video & audio: tools that generate video, music, and realistic voices (e.g. Sora, ElevenLabs).
  • Specialized assistants: Perplexity (AI search with sources), GitHub Copilot (coding), Notion AI (notes), and many niche apps.
  • Most of these are powered by the same kind of models you’re learning here — so your skills carry over.

Prompting mastery

◆
Outcome: the single highest-leverage skill in AI. You’ll learn the recipe, see the dramatic difference detail makes, and build prompts that get expert-level answers on the first or second try.

A prompt is just your message. But how you ask determines what you get. The gap between a beginner and a power user isn’t the tool — it’s the prompt.

🙁 Vague → generic, useless
write a business plan
— add the 4 ingredients —
😍 Specific → sharp, tailored
Act as a startup advisor. Write a one-page business plan for a small home bakery in Mumbai targeting office workers. Include sections for offering, customers, pricing, marketing, and first-month costs. Keep it practical and under 400 words.

The recipe: R-T-C-F

🎭 Role

Who it should be. “Act as a nutritionist…”

✅ Task

What to do. “…build a meal plan.”

📋 Context

The details. “Vegetarian, 1800 cal, no nuts.”

🎨 Format

How to present. “A 7-day table.”

Build one live 👇

Your prompt will appear here as you type…

The 5 habits of expert prompters

🔁
Iterate, don’t restart

Refine with follow-ups instead of rewriting from scratch.

“Make it shorter and more formal.”
❓
Let it ask you

Have it interview you before answering big tasks.

“Ask me 3 questions first, then begin.”
🪜
Request reasoning

For tricky problems, ask it to think step by step.

“Think it through step by step.”
🧾
Give it source material

Paste the text/data so it works from facts, not memory.

“Using only the text below, …”
🧠
Golden mindset: treat AI like a brilliant new teammate on day one — capable but lacking context. The more you tell it (who, what, why, for whom, in what style), the better it performs. Detailed in → excellent out.

Module 4 ~12 min · Core skill

The prompt patterns library

◆
Outcome: a toolbox of proven techniques you can reach for by name. Steal these — they work in any AI tool.
1

Role / Persona

Essential

Tell it who to be. This shifts vocabulary, depth, and tone instantly.

“Act as an experienced travel agent. Plan a budget 5-day Japan trip for a first-timer.”
2

Few-shot (show examples)

High impact

Give 1–3 examples of what “good” looks like, then ask for more in the same style.

“Here are two of my product taglines: ‘…’, ‘…’. Write 5 more in the same punchy style.”
3

Chain-of-thought (step by step)

For hard tasks

Ask it to reason before answering. Cuts mistakes on logic, math, and planning.

“A train problem… Work through it step by step, then give the final answer.”
4

Interview-me first

Power move

Flip it around so it gathers your context before producing anything.

“I want to write a wedding speech. Ask me questions one at a time, then write it.”
5

Reference text / grounding

Reduces errors

Paste the document, email, or data and restrict it to that source.

“Using only the report below, list the 3 key risks. [paste]”
6

Format & constraints

Everyday

Specify length, structure, and what to avoid.

“In a markdown table, max 5 rows, no jargon, plain English.”
7

Critique & improve

Quality boost

Make it review its own work — or yours.

“Critique this draft for clarity and tone, then rewrite the weakest parts.”
🚫
Common mistakes to avoid: being too vague · asking five things in one messy sentence · accepting the first draft · not giving it the source material · forgetting to say the format you want. Fix any one and your results jump.

Real-world workflows

◆

💼 At work

“Summarize these meeting notes into decisions, owners, and next steps.”

📊 Docs & reports

“Draft a project update with status, risks, and asks. Keep it to 200 words.”

🔎 Research

“Explain [topic] simply, then give pros, cons, and 3 sources to verify.”

📚 For learning

🎓 Personal tutor

“Teach me [topic] from scratch in 5 steps, checking I understand each one.”

🧪 Active recall

“Quiz me with 10 questions, one at a time, and explain my wrong answers.”

📖 Summarize & simplify

“Summarize this chapter, then explain it like I’m 12.”

🗓️ Study plans

“Build a 4-week plan to learn [skill], 30 min/day, with milestones.”

🏠 In daily life · 🎨 For creativity

🍽️ Plan & organize

Meal plans & shopping lists, trip itineraries, budgets, to-do breakdowns.

💬 Difficult writing

Tough emails, complaints, negotiations, apologies — drafted kindly and clearly.

✍️ Create

Stories, poems, captions, names, scripts, brainstorms — endless first drafts.

🧩 Decisions

“Help me weigh option A vs B for my situation: …”

🚀
The productivity principle: AI is best as a first-draft and thinking partner, not a final-word machine. Let it get you to 80% in seconds; you bring the judgment, accuracy, and personal touch for the last 20%.

Module 8 ~8 min · Frontier

Agents & automation — where it’s heading

◆
Outcome: understand the next wave — AI that doesn’t just answer, but acts — so you’re ready instead of surprised.

So far you’ve used AI as a conversation. The frontier is AI as an agent: give it a goal, and it plans, uses tools, and completes multi-step tasks largely on its own.

🤖

AI agents

Give a goal (“research these 5 cities and build a comparison”) and it carries out the steps itself.

🔌

Tools & connectors

AI that can browse, run code, query data, or connect to your apps (calendar, email, docs).

⚙️

Automation

Wiring AI into workflows so routine tasks happen automatically, not manually.

🧱 “Building” without being a coder

You can now describe an app or tool in plain English and have AI help create it. Non-coders are shipping real things.

🔭 What to watch

More autonomy, longer memory, better reliability, and AI woven into the apps you already use every day.

⚖️
Stay grounded: agents are powerful but still imperfect — they can take wrong steps confidently. The more autonomy you give, the more you should review what they do, especially for anything that costs money, sends messages, or changes files.

Module 9 ~10 min · Trust

Safety, ethics & truth

◆
Outcome: use AI responsibly and protect yourself — the habits that separate a savvy user from a careless one.

The five things that matter most

🔍 Verify facts

Treat factual claims as “probably right.” For anything important, confirm with a trusted source. Ask it for its sources.

🔒 Protect your data

Don’t paste passwords, financial details, IDs, or others’ private info. Check your tool’s privacy & training settings.

⚖️ Watch for bias

Models learn from human data and can reflect its biases. Be thoughtful with sensitive or high-stakes topics.

🧑‍⚖️ Keep human judgment

You’re accountable for what you use. AI advises; you decide — especially on health, legal, money, and people.

🌐
Misinformation & deepfakes: AI can generate convincing fake text, images, audio, and video. Be skeptical of sensational media, and be transparent when AI helped create something you share.

A simple responsible-use code

🪪

Be honest

Disclose AI use where it matters (work, school, publishing).

🧠

Stay sharp

Use it to think better, not to stop thinking.

🤝

Be fair

Respect privacy, copyright, and people’s dignity.

🛡️
The one-line rule: “If you wouldn’t put it on a postcard, don’t paste it in. If it really matters, verify it. And you — not the AI — are responsible for what you do with the answer.”

Glossary & FAQ

Every term you’ll hear, in plain English

AI

Software that performs tasks needing “intelligence” — here, understanding & generating language.

LLM

“Large Language Model” — the prediction engine behind chat assistants.

Prompt

The message/instruction you give the AI.

Token

A small chunk of text the model processes. Long inputs = more tokens.

Context window

How much it can “hold in mind” at once — its working memory.

Hallucination

A confident but false answer. Always verify facts.

Model

A specific AI “brain” version (e.g. Claude Opus, GPT-4-class). Newer ≈ smarter.

Multimodal

Handles more than text — images, audio, sometimes video.

Fine-tuning

Extra training to specialize a model for a task or style.

Agent

AI that takes actions and completes multi-step tasks, not just chats.

RAG

“Retrieval” — feeding the AI your documents so answers are grounded in them.

Knowledge cutoff

The date its built-in knowledge stops (web search gets around it).

Questions everyone asks

Q Do I need to be technical?
No. If you can send a text, you can use AI — you just talk to it in plain language. This whole cohort assumes zero background.
Q Is it free, and is it safe to use?
All major tools have capable free tiers. They’re safe for everyday use if you follow Module 9: don’t share secrets, verify important facts, and check your privacy settings.
Q Why does it sometimes get things wrong?
Because it predicts plausible text rather than looking up verified facts (Module 1). It’s superb at language, imperfect at precise facts — so you verify what matters.
Q Will AI take my job?
It’s more likely that people who use AI well will outpace those who don’t. The skill you built today is exactly that edge.
Q Which single tool should a beginner pick?
Any major one — Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini. Get fluent in one; the skills transfer. Switch later if you want.
Q How do I keep up as it changes fast?
You don’t need to chase everything. Master the fundamentals (done!), then try one new feature a week. Fundamentals barely change; features just get better.
